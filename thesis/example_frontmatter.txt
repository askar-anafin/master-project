Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1 B ackground and Clinical Context
Scoliosis is a structural deformity of the spine characterised by a lateral curvature
exceeding 10 degrees as measured by the Cobb angle on an anteroposterior radiograph
[1]. The condition affects approximately 2–3% of the adolescent population [2]. The
Cobb angle is the primary quantitative criterion for grading severity, selecting treatment,
and monitoring progression [3]. Curves below 25 degrees are typically managed through
observation, curves between 25 and 45 degrees may require bracing, and curves exceeding
45 degrees are candidates for surgical correction [4]. A measurement error of 5 degrees
can shift a patient across these treatment boundaries.
The standard measurement procedure requires a specialist to identify the most tilted
vertebrae at the boundaries of each spinal curve, draw lines along their endplates, and
measure the angle between these lines. This process is manual, time-consuming, and
subject to observer variability. Reported inter-observer differences range from 3 to 5
degrees, and intra-observer differences are approximately 3 degrees [5–7]. These figures
define a practical benchmark: an automated method should produce errors comparable
to or smaller than the disagreement between experts.
Deep learning has produced encouraging results in this domain. Convolutional net-
works trained on annotated radiographs can localise vertebral landmarks and estimate
Cobb angles with errors approaching those of human observers on certain benchmarks
[8–10]. Two limitations persist, however. First, most published systems operate on images
downsampled to 512×512 pixels or below, discarding spatial detail relevant to precise

1.2 C urrent State of the Problem
Automated Cobb angle estimation has been approached through several methodological
families. Classification-based methods assign images to discrete severity categories but
do not produce continuous angle values [14]. Segmentation-based methods produce pixel-
level vertebral masks from which angles can be derived geometrically [15, 16]. Landmark
detection methods predict anatomical point coordinates and compute angles from their
spatial relationships [11, 12, 17]. Direct regression methods predict Cobb angles as
continuous outputs without explicit geometric intermediaries [18].
Reported mean absolute errors on the AASCE benchmark range from approximately
3 to 5 degrees for competitive methods [11]. Three observations characterise the current
state of the field. First, the majority of methods process images at 512×512 pixels or lower.
Second, few methods explicitly combine direct regional angle regression with geometric
information derived from vertebral landmarks or tilts. Third, prediction reliability has
received limited attention in the scoliosis literature despite its relevance for clinical
adoption [13].
This thesis focuses on these three issues: image resolution, integration of direct and
geometry-derived angle signals, and explicit reporting of model uncertainty.
1.3 R esearch Aim, Objectives, and Hypothesis
The aim of this work is to develop and experimentally evaluate a multi-task neural
network for automated Cobb angle estimation from spinal radiographs, incorporating
dual-path angle prediction and uncertainty-aware outputs.
The following objectives were defined:
1. Conduct a critical review of existing approaches to automated scoliosis assessment.
The review covers deep learning architectures, multi-task learning strategies, and
methods for prediction reliability.

2. Design the DualPathNet architecture. The design combines global feature extraction
through adaptive pooling with local feature extraction at predicted vertebral
locations. A learned gating mechanism combines direct regional angle predictions
with geometry-derived auxiliary angle signals.
3. Formulate a composite loss function with coordinate-loss scale normalisation. The
loss jointly supervises vertebral centre detection, corner keypoint regression, per-
vertebra tilt prediction, regional Cobb angle regression, vertebra count classification,
and fused-output uncertainty prediction.
4. Implement a curriculum learning training pipeline with configurable phase transi-
tions, progressively introducing angle supervision, reliability weighting, and hard-
sample mining.
5. Evaluate the trained model on the SpinalAI2024 dataset with leakage-mitigated
splits. The evaluation compares baseline configurations, ablation variants, and
published methods. It also assesses cross-domain adaptability through zero-shot
transfer and fine-tuning on the independent AASCE19 clinical dataset.
The primary hypothesis is that a dual-path multi-task neural network can achieve a
mean absolute error below 2.5 degrees on leakage-mitigated SpinalAI2024 validation and
holdout splits under the regional PT/MT/TL-L evaluation protocol. This hypothesis
concerns the combination of direct regional Cobb angle regression with geometry-derived
auxiliary signals from vertebral tilt predictions. A secondary hypothesis is that model
uncertainty can rank high-error cases sufficiently well to support selective expert review.
An exploratory hypothesis is that the architecture can be adapted to a clinical target
domain through limited fine-tuning at a low learning rate. Zero-shot clinical generalisation
is expected to remain constrained by synthetic-to-clinical domain shift.

and geometry-derived angle signals, coordinate-loss scale normalisation, and per-angle
