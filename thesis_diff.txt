-\section{Background and Clinical Context}
-Cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) remain the leading cause of mortality globally, accounting for an estimated 17.9 million deaths annually according to the World Health Organization. Electrocardiography (ECG) is the primary, non-invasive diagnostic modality used to detect cardiac abnormalities such as arrhythmias, myocardial infarction, conduction blocks, and chamber hypertrophy. Standard clinical practice relies heavily on visual interpretation of 12-lead ECG waveforms by trained cardiologists. However, this process is prone to human error, subjective interpretation, and substantial inter-observer variability. In emergency and rural healthcare settings, the lack of immediate access to specialized cardiologists can lead to critical delays in diagnosis and treatment.
-
-\section{Current State of the Problem}
-Computer-aided electrocardiogram classification systems offer a viable solution by providing real-time, automated triage and decision support. The primary challenge in current computer-aided ECG analysis is the limitation of traditional machine learning, which relies on manual feature engineering. While deep learning has bypassed this by extracting features automatically, standard models (like CNNs) treat the 12 leads as simple independent channels. This omits the structural and physical spatial geometry of the electrode placements. Furthermore, many models act as "black boxes," providing predictions without clinical interpretability or uncertainty quantification. Clinical adoption requires models that can model inter-lead relationships, provide explainable outputs, and flag ambiguous predictions for expert review.
-

+\section{Cardiac Electrophysiology and the Physiological Basis of Standard 12-Lead Electrocardiography}
 The mammalian heart is a complex muscular organ regulated by a highly specialized intrinsic electrical conduction system. The cardiac cycle is initiated by the spontaneous depolarization of pacemaker cells located in the sinoatrial (SA) node, situated in the posterior wall of the right atrium. These specialized cells generate action potentials without neural input due to a slow, inward sodium current known as the pacemaker current (funny current, $I_f$). Once an action potential is generated, it propagates rapidly through the atrial myocardium via the internodal pathways, initiating atrial depolarization and subsequent mechanical contraction, which is clinically represented as the P wave on the surface electrocardiogram (ECG) \cite{schlapfer2017ecg}.
 
 The electrical impulse then converges on the atrioventricular (AV) node, located at the junction of the atria and ventricles. The AV node introduces a critical physiological delay of approximately 100 ms. This delay is anatomically essential because it allows the atria to complete mechanical contraction (atrial kick) and fully fill the ventricles with blood before ventricular contraction begins. Following this delay, the electrical impulse enters the Bundle of His, which splits into the left bundle branch (which further divides into anterior and posterior fascicles) and the right bundle branch. These branches terminate in the Purkinje fiber network, a dense system of fast-conducting subendocardial fibers that distribute the depolarization wave to the ventricular myocardium. Ventricular depolarization occurs from the apex of the heart upward toward the base, manifesting as the high-amplitude QRS complex on the ECG. Ventricular repolarization follows shortly after, producing the T wave. The electrical cycle concludes with a brief period of resting potential before the next SA node discharge \cite{schlapfer2017ecg, goldberger2000physiobank}.

+\section{Information Fusion via Spatio-Temporal Hybrid Networks Integrating Convolutional and Self-Attention Blocks}
+The Spatio-Temporal Hybrid Model (ResNet-Transformer) integrates convolutional layers with a transformer encoder to exploit the morphological feature extraction efficiency of convolutions alongside the global temporal modeling capabilities of self-attention. This design successfully addresses the data-hungry nature of pure vision transformers when trained on medium-scale datasets such as PTB-XL by reducing the temporal sequence length and establishing stable local token boundaries prior to self-attention computation. The overall structural data flow of the hybrid model is visualized in Figure \ref{fig:hybrid_flow}, and the detailed hyperparameter configuration for each layer is detailed in Table \ref{tab:hybrid_arch}.
+
+The model architecture is organized into three primary sequential phases:
+\begin{enumerate}
+    \item \textbf{1D Convolutional Front-End:} The raw 12-lead ECG signal of shape $(12, 5000)$ is first processed by an initial 1D convolution layer with a large kernel size ($k=15$, stride $s=2$) and batch normalization to capture local high-frequency waveforms. It is then downsampled via a max pooling layer ($k=3$, stride $s=2$). The signal is subsequently propagated through three 1D ResNet stages:
+    \begin{itemize}
+        \item \emph{Stage 1:} Stacks two residual blocks with 64 filters and stride 1, preserving the temporal sequence length of 1250.
+        \item \emph{Stage 2:} Stacks two residual blocks with 128 filters and stride 2, downsampling the sequence length to 625.
+        \item \emph{Stage 3:} Stacks two residual blocks with 256 filters and stride 2, downsampling the sequence length to 313.
+    \end{itemize}
+    This front-end reduces the sequence length from $5000$ to $313$, acting as a highly efficient downsampling and local feature extraction module.
+    
+    \item \textbf{Squeeze-and-Excitation Lead Attention:} After the final ResNet stage, a Squeeze-and-Excitation (SE) block is applied to model inter-channel (inter-lead) dependencies. Let $X \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times L}$ be the output feature map, where $C = 256$ channels and $L = 313$ sequence length. A global average pooling operation computes the channel descriptor $z \in \mathbb{R}^C$:
+    \begin{equation}
+        z_c = \frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} X_c(i)
+    \end{equation}
+    To capture channel-wise dependencies, a gating mechanism with a sigmoid activation is applied:
+    \begin{equation}
+        s = \sigma(W_2 \cdot \text{ReLU}(W_1 \cdot z))
+    \end{equation}
+    where $W_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{\frac{C}{r} \times C}$ and $W_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times \frac{C}{r}}$ represent fully connected layers, with a channel reduction ratio $r = 16$. The final output of the block is computed by scaling the input feature map $X$ with the activation vector $s$:
+    \begin{equation}
+        \tilde{X}_c = s_c \cdot X_c
+    \end{equation}
+    The channels are then projected into the model dimension $D_{model} = 256$ using a 1D convolution with kernel size 1.
+    
+    \item \textbf{Transformer Encoder Back-End:} The channel-adjusted sequence of shape $(256, 313)$ is transposed to shape $(313, 256)$. A learnable classification token $x_{cls} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times 256}$ is prepended, and 1D positional embeddings $E_{pos} \in \mathbb{R}^{314 \times 256}$ are added:
+    \begin{equation}
+        Z_0 = [x_{cls}; \tilde{X}] + E_{pos}
+    \end{equation}
+    where $Z_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 314 \times 256}$. The sequence is then processed by $N = 3$ Transformer encoder layers. Each layer consists of Multi-Head Self-Attention (MHA) with 8 heads and a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) block with a feedforward dimension $d_{ff} = 512$ and dropout of 0.1. The classification representation at index 0 (the CLS token) is extracted, normalized, and classified using a 2-layer MLP classifier:
+    \begin{equation}
+        y = W_4 \cdot \text{ReLU}(W_3 \cdot \text{LayerNorm}(Z_{N, 0}))
+    \end{equation}
+\end{enumerate}

+\section{Decision Boundary Threshold Calibration and Inductive Bias Blending via Soft Voting Ensembles}
+While individual deep learning models demonstrate strong diagnostic performance, clinical deployment requires maximizing the sensitivity and specificity of predictions. We investigate two post-hoc strategies to further enhance the classification metrics without retraining: class-specific validation threshold optimization and soft voting ensembling.
+
+First, standard classification uses a default decision threshold of $0.5$ for all diagnostic classes. However, due to severe class imbalance (e.g., normal sinus rhythm is vastly more frequent than hypertrophy), a static threshold leads to suboptimal performance on minority classes. We optimize class-specific thresholds on the validation partition using a grid search over the range $[0.01, 0.99]$ with a step size of $0.01$ to maximize the F1-score for each individual class:
+\begin{equation}
+    \text{threshold}_c = \arg\max_{t} F_1(y_{\text{val}, c}, \hat{y}_{\text{val}, c} > t)
+\end{equation}
+where $y_{\text{val}, c}$ represents the validation labels and $\hat{y}_{\text{val}, c}$ is the predicted probability for class $c$.

+\begin{equation}
+    P_{\text{ensemble}} = \frac{1}{3} \left( P_{\text{cnn}} + P_{\text{gnn}} + P_{\text{hybrid}} \right)
+\end{equation}

+The physiological logic behind ensembling lies in its ability to \textbf{blend complementary architectural inductive biases}:
+\begin{itemize}
+    \item \textbf{CNN (1D ResNet-18)} provides a strong morphological bias, excelling at detecting localized waveforms such as acute ST-segment elevations (MI) or sharp T-wave inversions (STTC).
+    \item \textbf{GNN (ST-ReGE)} models the anatomical lead layout, excelling at capturing spatial correlations (CD, HYP) across contiguous leads.
+    \item \textbf{Spatio-Temporal Hybrid} models the global rhythm context, leveraging self-attention to capture long-range sequential relationships across different beats.
+\end{itemize}

+

+
+Choosing \textbf{post-hoc decision boundary calibration (validation-set grid search)} over other optimization methods, such as cost-sensitive learning (e.g., BCE weighted loss or minority class oversampling), is supported by several critical advantages:
+\begin{enumerate}
+    \item \textbf{Preserved Rank Ordering:} Loss-level weighting during training alters the gradients and can destabilize optimization, often causing the model to over-predict minority classes blindly and distorting the rank-ordering capability (AUPRC). In contrast, post-hoc calibration works strictly at the decision boundary, preserving the model's underlying discriminative rank-ordering while calibrating the output for clinical screening.
+    \item \textbf{Non-Retraining Efficiency:} Retraining deep PyTorch models is computationally expensive (requiring GPUs and hours of training) and sensitive to hyperparameter choices. Validation-set grid search is a lightweight operation completed in seconds on a CPU, allowing clinicians to dynamically shift thresholds in real-time based on the desired screening sensitivity (e.g., prioritizing recall during outbreak triage or precision during secondary diagnosis) without altering model weights.
+    \item \textbf{Robustness to Test-Set Shift:} Calibrating on a distinct validation partition prevents the model from overfitting to the training distribution, resulting in decision boundaries that generalize much better to the held-out test partition.
+\end{enumerate}
+These findings demonstrate that combined ensembling and validation threshold calibration form the essential components for high-accuracy, trustworthy clinical decision support.
